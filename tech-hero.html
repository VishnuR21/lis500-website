Tech Hero

Tim Berners‑Lee

Inventor of the World Wide Web and advocate for an open, user‑centered internet.

Profile

Tim Berners-Lee is the computer scientist who invented the World Wide Web. In 1989, while working at CERN, he came up with the idea of linking and sharing information across different computers. He created the first web browser and web server, and he made the main web technologies like HTML and HTTP free for anyone to use. Because he chose not to keep it private or charge for it, people all over the world were able to build websites and contribute to the internet as we know it.

His leadership matters for equity because keeping the web open lowered barriers to entry. Schools, small organizations, and individuals did not need special permission or large amounts of money to create something online. He also started the World Wide Web Consortium, which brings together different groups to agree on web standards. This helps make sure the web works across devices and for different communities, instead of being controlled by just one company.

More recently, Berners-Lee has spoken out about how much power and personal data are now controlled by a small number of companies. He has argued that the web should give users more control over their own information and better protect privacy. Through projects like Solid, he has focused on the idea that people should own their data and decide who can access it. In a class like LIS 500, he is a good example of how design choices in technology can either open access and spread power more evenly, or concentrate power in the hands of a few.
